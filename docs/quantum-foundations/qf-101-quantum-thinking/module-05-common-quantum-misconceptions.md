# Module 5: Common Quantum Misconceptions

## Purpose

This module helps learners recognize common beginner misconceptions. Correcting these early makes later quantum learning more reliable.

## Misconception 1: Quantum Computers Are Faster At Everything

Quantum computers are not faster for every task. Quantum speedups depend on the problem, the algorithm, the hardware, and the cost of preparing and measuring the system.

Some tasks may benefit from quantum algorithms. Many everyday tasks are still better handled by classical computers.

## Misconception 2: Simulation Is The Same As Hardware Execution

A simulator runs a mathematical model on a classical computer. Quantum hardware runs experiments on physical quantum systems.

Simulators are valuable for learning and testing ideas, but they do not capture every hardware limitation. Hardware can involve noise, calibration issues, connectivity limits, and other practical constraints.

## Misconception 3: Entanglement Is Instant Messaging

Entanglement is a real quantum relationship, but it is not a way to send ordinary messages faster than light.

This course does not require entanglement yet. The important beginner point is to avoid treating dramatic phrases as explanations.

## Misconception 4: Small Samples Always Show The True Pattern

Small shot counts can mislead. A 10-shot run can look uneven even when a larger sample would look more balanced.

This is why learners record shot counts and avoid overinterpreting tiny samples.

## Misconception 5: Measurement Is Ordinary Looking

Quantum measurement is not just "looking" in the everyday sense. Measurement is an operation that produces classical outcomes from a quantum system.

In beginner experiments, the details are simplified, but learners should still treat measurement as part of the experiment design.

## Mini Task

Choose one misconception and correct it in plain language.

Write for another beginner. Do not use hype or dramatic claims.

## Vocabulary

- **Misconception**: A misleading or incorrect idea.
- **Simulator**: Classical software used to model quantum circuits.
- **Hardware execution**: Running an experiment on physical quantum hardware.
- **Noise**: Unwanted effects that can disturb a quantum experiment.
